So specifically, things like Visual Studio, and Azure.   • Just playing games as a kid. Basically, you know, I remember distinctly playing Megaman where they have the boss robots at the end.   • Well, what sort of started your your coding or programming journey?
  • Let's see, I originally in school was always, you know, more into like English and Social Studies, as opposed to like math and back then, you know, computer programming was a lot less accessible. We certainly didn't have it in school until college, you know. It was also, like, the technologies itself was less accessible, it's more more cryptic, less user friendly.
  • Really, the game industry kind of worked, like how I'm about to describe, where designers were did more documentation, and writing, and communication, and less creating assets. That started to change and, as I started to realize, "okay, you have to make like a portfolio to get a job in the game industry and in the 21st century." This was before, Unity and Unreal, were really things you could just download. It was before YouTube, to be honest. I've seen a lot of weird changes over time in terms of that, but I just was very hard to discover how you actually made a game.
  • Then, eventually, I met somebody in grad school, who is a really motivated and talented programmer who made his own game engine called a "Flat Red Ball." It's still a thing, It's open source, It's mainly a 2D Engine now. He, sort of, introduced programming to me and showed me how it was relevant to video games.
  • Really, the game engine thing was the missing link. Because when you just sit down, you're like, "Well, I guess I have to like figure out rendering and figure out all this stuff that is like insane to try to program on your own." Unless you're really talented programmer, which, you know, that wasn't my goal. So rather than trying to have to make your own game engine, being able to like start with the game engine was a game changer.

And it was like, after my first job as a game designer, and I started trying to get more into the indie space. It just became more and more obvious that programming, you know, like, none of my indie games made really much money at all. So I've kind of transitioned into more of a technical role over the years. Just out of necessity, I guess.

  • So one of my grenade questions? Why do all modern games indie and AAA seem to share the same pillars of gameplay? Can new game gameplay pillars be found in the game industry in game development?
  • Well, I think there's two big answers I have to that. One is, is that because of the accessibility we just discussed, tons and tons of games are getting made, it's not super common to expect, you're going to come up with something that no one else has ever thought of yet. It's just a factor of living in the 21st century of postmodern condition type thing. I don't think that's bad. I think that's just realistic.
  • There's still lots of new opportunities to mix ideas, you know, work within, quote unquote pastiche, like blends of different genres, which we've been seeing in the game industry for a long time. And so I don't think that's like, because people aren't creative. I just think that's because so many games have been made, it's not easy to come up with new ideas, or 100% original ideas. I don't think that matters.
  • I also think another factor is going back to economics. Especially in AAA, you know, games are investments, people who fund them are trying to get a return on their investment. And so if you are an investor, you want a safe investment. And we know, you know, Halo 6 is a safe bet. Halo 6 is a safe bet to make X amount of money, then we're gonna make Halo 6, basically change as little as we can get away with in order to make that safe investment return money.
  • In the indie space, that's pretty much why you see more innovation. Because there, there's less folks investments at risk. So the indies that make a lot of money are like the unicorns, weird superstars, like rock star stories, those are the exceptions. If you want to make money in indies, most people have to make a lot of stuff really fast, and operate within popular genres that have built in audiences. Otherwise, it's gonna be hard. So that's the explanation.

But it's called gonna kind of be derived from a lot of the work that other people have done in the past. And I think that's natural.

So I think whenever you have something like that, you'll feel sort of quick burst of new thought, like new ideas, but the core genres, they're mainly still the same.

  • For me, I'm a little bothered by how, like, commercialized games have become, you know, there's this concept of like, is it worth buying? And I also think it's difficult that so many folks want to create in this space, but aren't able to because of the steep barriers to entry, basically, relating to supply and demand economics again.
  • You know, there's like, so many young people who want to work in the game industry and only so many jobs. So, to me, what I'm hopeful for are more opportunities for people to share their work and actually find audiences. And so what I'm personally working on is like a local in person, space for that.
  • I think if you just put your thing on the internet, it's just like such a huge world that the odds of folks finding it are very difficult. Especially when you're competing with like influencers, and AAA. and all the marketing dollars. So I think local, more community oriented game circles, and opportunities for games, and public spaces. That's what I am excited about and what I'm trying to work on.
