Take Away

Jacob Nader provided me with a unique perspective. He is a budding young professional with questions about his place within the industry and a desire to understand, or change, its status quo, we are peers on common ground. We certainly share many of the same perspectives and feelings, but he still left with many new thoughts and ideas: Innovation can be as much an alienator as it can be an attractor. Rehashes become the standards that will be rehashed in the future.

My research and exploration of stagnation in the video game genre is, in many ways, a demand for innovation or a demand for something greater than before, but Nader provided me with many examples as to why innovation is not always the strongest move, or even required in some case, and to be careful of what I wish for. Nader grew up playing the Zelda and Halo franchises; Two legacy IPs from major established studios, and he has watched them evolve and iterate over the years as the landscape and hardware of video games has done the same. While Nader enjoyed some of the ways these IPs have evolved from their original concepts and formulas, not every change was warranted or effective in his opinion. Nader states his opinion on the Zelda title, Breath of the Wild move from linear to open world, “I definitely feel like it alienated a lot of people going from linear to open world. But I also really sort of liked how they executed it. It's just not really what I play Zelda for. --- I don't like the way that the dungeons were executed in that one either. They felt a lot A lot less complex. --- there was not a lot of gear that you would pick up --- that's something that Zelda is known for is having those cool gadgets. --- there was a story the collecting the tools, in most cases, like you could tell from the area that you were in ---.”

Nader also made an extremely beautiful and eloquent statement about his opinions and feelings on the ability of games to develop new pillars of gameplay, leaving me at a loss for words, that wonderfully encapsulates how different genres, or anything else really, are built upon that which came before. “I feel like we will always see these similar looking pillars in the halls of games, but there will always be like a new motif on them, there always be a new design that goes along with the pillar. So while the pillar itself might not change, it's the stuff on the pillar that will change. And that's a good way to go about it, because eventually, we'll get to a point where the pillar is still there. But it looks entirely different. Now we have a new type of game. Thus creating an entirely new pillar in the process starts again, people start taking from one thing they like, putting it on that pillar, that another person that played that game gets in the animation, or a game industry puts their own spin on the pillar that keeps changing somewhere, and so forth.”
