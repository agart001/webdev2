Take Away

Justin Santiago is, at his core, truly a gamer’s gamer. A man who truly loves the medium with all his heart and someone I trust to give an honest opinion when it is necessary, our interview proved that without a shadow of a doubt. As a person who has played video games at every stage of his life, Santiago understands what makes or breaks a game, what makes it a worthwhile investment of time, and what makes a player want to keep returning to play more. Santiago taught me that: Gamers value directed player agency. Gamers are willing to accept tasteful microtransactions or secondary purchases.

Santiago is a gamer who values his freedom. Games are a form of escapism and entertainment, Santiago would rather a game allows him the freedom to choose how he would like to employ that escapism and entertainment. While freedom is a powerful tool when designing a game, it can, however, deteriorate the experience, especially when the freedom is not directed in an effective manner. Directed freedom? Seems like an oxymoron, and it is, but in practice, directed freedom is a method of allowing player agency while also subtly puppeteering them towards fulfilling aspects of the game. Skyrim and Elden Ring, Santiago and I discussed, are games which expertly direct player agency, each game allows the player to go where they want, be who they want, and do what they want, while also subtly pointing them towards an end goal through their level design and story. Conversely, a game that, due to a lack of directed freedom, dissatisfied Santiago was Hollow, he stated, “It just felt like lacking. And I didn't like the lack of direction. It, like, doesn't give you much. --- they can just throw you in. And it took me like, For, I mean, two hours just to get through like this first part and I was like nope. And I haven't touched the game since.”

Santiago is a reasonable man, a reasonable gamer if you will, and is willing to spend money on things that he deems have reasonable value, things that are not overzealous, predatory, or downright nonsensical. Santiago, like many gamers, is willing to spend money on a game after his initial purchase, but if he is going to fork over his hard-earned money it must be tasteful and worthwhile. The development of games is only getting more costly and more costly; Most gamers, like Santiago, understand that developers need to supplement their games with microtransactions or DLC to account for their overhead. An issue arises, however, when development studios, usually at the behest of their publishers, fill their games with lackluster microtransactions or cut their game into pieces to be slowly auctioned out as seasonal content. Santiago’s opinion is made very clear in his statement, “I feel like some companies do it just right and some don't. Like, it's almost like, hey, we're gonna drop this game. This, you know, it feels partially complete. We're just gonna drop some more stuff along. The way and then add some DLCs that are essential if you want to progress, but you know, that's it. And that I don't like that much. But like some games do get it right. But I feel like that's typically just like multiplayer free to play games ---.”
